# Lesson 10: Pricing, ROI, and Commercial Models

## Objective

Use the learner's accounting background to build pricing confidence and commercial discipline.

This is where the learner should become especially strong.

## Pricing is strategy

Price is not just a number. It communicates:

- Value
- Positioning
- Customer segment
- Confidence
- Scope
- Support level
- Market maturity

A weak salesperson fears price. A strong commercial co-founder understands and explains it.

## Common SaaS pricing models

### Monthly subscription

Customer pays every month.

Good for:

- CRM
- Dashboards
- Sales tools
- Operations platforms

### Annual subscription

Customer pays yearly, often at a discount.

Good for:

- Serious customers
- Budget planning
- Reducing churn risk

### Setup fee

One-time payment for onboarding, configuration, training, data import, or integration.

Good for:

- Businesses requiring support
- Enterprise onboarding
- Custom setup

### Usage-based pricing

Customer pays based on usage.

Examples:

- Per SMS
- Per API call
- Per transaction
- Per customer record

### Per-user pricing

Customer pays by number of staff users.

Good when user count reflects product value.

### Per-branch pricing

Customer pays based on locations or branches.

Good for multi-location businesses.

### Tiered pricing

Different packages for different levels.

Example:

- Starter
- Growth
- Business
- Enterprise

## Pricing design questions

Ask:

- Who gets the most value?
- What metric grows as the customer grows?
- What costs increase with usage?
- What support is required?
- What price feels serious but reachable?
- What is the cost of the customer's current problem?
- How does pricing change by segment?

## ROI framework

Use this simple structure:

```text
Revenue gained + Cost saved + Risk reduced - Product cost = Net business value
```

Example:

A gas company has 2,000 customers.

If better reminders bring back 100 customers monthly and each creates $3 profit:

```text
100 x $3 = $300 monthly profit
```

If the software costs $150 monthly:

```text
$300 - $150 = $150 estimated net value
```

This is not a guarantee. It is a business case.

## Value-based pricing

Do not only price based on your cost. Price based on value created.

Cost-based thinking:

> Hosting costs us $20, so we can charge $50.

Value-based thinking:

> If the product helps the customer recover $1,000 in lost revenue monthly, a $250 monthly subscription can be reasonable.

## Pilot pricing

A pilot should answer:

- Can the customer use the product?
- Does the product solve the pain?
- Can value be proven?
- Should the customer expand?

Pilot structure:

```text
Duration: 30 days
Scope: customer records + messaging + reporting
Users: 3 staff
Success metric: 500 customers uploaded, 2 campaigns sent, 5% increase in repeat engagement
Price: discounted pilot or setup fee
Decision date: fixed
Post-pilot price: agreed before pilot starts
```

## Discount rules

Never discount without reason.

Acceptable reasons:

- Annual upfront payment
- Reduced scope
- Strategic logo
- Referral commitment
- Case study permission
- Longer contract

Bad reasons:

- Fear
- Weak confidence
- Customer pressure
- No clear value explanation
