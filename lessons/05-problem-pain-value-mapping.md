# Lesson 05: Problem, Pain, and Value Mapping

## Objective

Teach the learner how to connect customer pain to measurable business value.

Sales becomes powerful when the customer sees that the problem is not just annoying but expensive.

## Core framework

Use this structure:

```text
Problem -> Pain -> Cost -> Solution -> Outcome -> Proof
```

Example:

```text
Problem: Customers are not followed up after purchase.
Pain: Customers forget to return or buy from competitors.
Cost: Lost repeat sales and weak customer loyalty.
Solution: Customer records, segmentation, and automated reminders.
Outcome: More repeat purchases and better visibility.
Proof: Track reminders sent, returning customers, and repeat sales.
```

## Difference between problem and pain

A problem is the issue.

Pain is the emotional, financial, or operational consequence.

Problem:

> Customer records are not organized.

Pain:

> The owner cannot target loyal customers, staff waste time searching records, and customers are not followed up.

## Types of pain

### Financial pain

- Lost revenue
- High costs
- Poor cash flow
- Leakage
- Wasted spending

### Operational pain

- Manual work
- Slow reporting
- Repeated errors
- Staff confusion
- Poor handover

### Customer pain

- Poor follow-up
- Bad service experience
- No reminders
- No loyalty recognition

### Management pain

- No visibility
- No accountability
- Weak controls
- Delayed decisions

### Growth pain

- Cannot scale branches
- Cannot track sales team
- Cannot personalize marketing
- Cannot measure campaigns

## Value mapping table

| Pain | Business cost | Product capability | Measurable outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| Customers do not return | Lost repeat revenue | Reminders and campaigns | Repeat purchase rate |
| Records scattered | Time wasted and errors | Central customer database | Faster lookup, fewer duplicates |
| Reports delayed | Slow decisions | Dashboard | Daily visibility |
| Staff actions unclear | Weak accountability | User roles and audit trail | Traceable activity |
| Manual messaging | Time wasted | Bulk messaging | Faster outreach |

## How accounting helps here

An accountant can ask sharper value questions:

- What is the monthly revenue impact?
- What percentage of customers repeat?
- What is the average transaction value?
- What is the cost of one lost customer?
- How much time does staff spend manually preparing reports?
- How much do errors cost?
- What does delayed reporting affect?

This makes the salesperson sound serious.

## ROI thinking

Return on investment does not always need to be exact. But it must be reasonable.

Simple formula:

```text
Estimated Monthly Value Created - Monthly Product Cost = Net Value
```

Example:

If a business gains 20 extra repeat customers monthly and each customer brings $10 profit:

```text
20 x $10 = $200 extra monthly profit
```

If the software costs $75 monthly:

```text
$200 - $75 = $125 estimated net value
```

The point is not to manipulate numbers. The point is to help the customer see the business case.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Build a pain map

Choose one customer segment and create a table with:

- Problems
- Pain
- Cost
- Product solution
- Proof metric

### Exercise 2: Ask cost questions

Create 10 questions that reveal financial cost.

Examples:

- How many customers do not return monthly?
- What is the average profit per repeat customer?
- How many hours does staff spend on manual follow-up?

### Exercise 3: Convert weak pitch to value pitch

Weak pitch:

> We provide a CRM and SMS platform.

Rewrite using:

```text
Problem -> Cost -> Solution -> Outcome
```
