# Lesson 09: Objection Handling and Negotiation

## Objective

You will learn how to handle objections without becoming defensive, desperate, or weak on value.

An objection is not rejection. It is information.

## Objection handling mindset

Do not argue. Diagnose.

When a customer objects, ask:

- Is this a real blocker or a surface excuse?
- Is it about price, trust, timing, authority, product fit, or change management?
- What must be true for them to move forward?

## Common objections

### Objection 1: It is expensive

Weak response:

> We can reduce the price.

Strong response:

> I understand. Let us compare the price to the cost of the problem. If the system helps bring back even a small number of customers monthly, what would that be worth to the business?

Follow-up questions:

- Expensive compared to what?
- What budget did you have in mind?
- What would make the price feel justified?
- What result would you need to see in the first month?

### Objection 2: We already use Excel

Strong response:

> Excel is useful for storing information, but it does not automate follow-up, track customer activity in real time, send reminders, or show live business performance. The question is whether Excel is enough for where the business is going.

### Objection 3: My staff will not use it

Strong response:

> That is a real concern. Adoption is usually the biggest risk with business software. That is why we should start with one simple workflow, train the team, and measure usage before expanding.

### Objection 4: Let me think about it

Strong response:

> That makes sense. So I can support you properly, what exactly do you need to think through: price, timing, staff adoption, or whether it fits your process?

### Objection 5: We do not have budget now

Strong response:

> Understood. Is the issue that there is no budget at all, or that the value is not yet clear enough to allocate budget?

### Objection 6: We need custom features

Strong response:

> That may be possible, but before we discuss customization, let us separate what is required to start from what would be useful later. What must be available for the first successful pilot?

### Objection 7: We are not ready

Strong response:

> What would need to be in place for you to be ready?

## Negotiation principles

### 1. Do not discount without exchange

If you reduce price, get something in return:

- Longer contract
- Upfront payment
- Case study
- Referral
- Reduced scope
- Faster decision

### 2. Separate price from scope

If the customer wants a lower price, reduce scope, not value.

Example:

> We can start with the customer and messaging module only, then add reporting later.

### 3. Sell pilot with success metrics

A pilot should not be vague.

Define:

- Duration
- Users
- Features
- Success metric
- Support
- Decision date
- Next price after pilot

### 4. Protect the product's value

Discounting too quickly teaches the customer that your first price was not real.

## Objection diagnosis table

| Objection | Possible real meaning | Good question |
|---|---|---|
| Too expensive | Value not clear | What result would justify the price? |
| Let me think | Hidden blocker | What part do you need to think through? |
| Staff will not use it | Adoption risk | Which workflow would be easiest to start with? |
| We use Excel | Current workaround | What does Excel fail to do for you today? |
| No budget | Not urgent enough | What problems currently receive budget? |
| Need custom features | Scope confusion | What is required for the first pilot? |

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Objection bank

Create a list of 20 objections you expect customers to raise.

For each, write:

- Surface objection
- Possible real issue
- Clarifying question
- Response
- Next step

### Exercise 2: Role play

Practice with a partner. The partner must object aggressively. Your goal is not to win. Your goal is to stay calm and diagnose.

### Exercise 3: Discount discipline

Create three alternatives to discounting:

- Reduced scope
- Longer contract
- Pilot package
